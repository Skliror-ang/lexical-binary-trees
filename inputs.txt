The Evolution of the Shortest Path Problem: A Comparison of Introductory Treatment and Current Research 

The CS Team


Course: Academic Discourse in English for Engineers 
Course instructor: Goni Togia 

Athens 2026
Abstract 
The shortest path problem is a central topic in computer science, where the identification of weight-minimizing paths in graphs is well documented in the introductory literature. While coursebooks present the problem as a resolved task solved by classical algorithms such as Dijkstra’s and Bellman–Ford, less attention is given to how recent research questions the limit of time complexity these algorithms have long been assumed to set. A comparative analysis was conducted across four sources, including two coursebooks, one review article, and one research paper, in order to examine how treatment of the shortest path problem differs between introductory and research levels. The comparison indicates a progression from a static algorithmic problem in the coursebooks to an active research area in which both hardness results for constrained variants and faster algorithms for the directed single-source case are still being established. Across the four sources, treatment varied from formal proofs of correctness and worst-case analysis to deterministic algorithms that bypass the traditional sorting-based barrier. The comparison shows that introductory treatment frames the shortest path problem as resolved by Dijkstra's and Bellman–Ford algorithms, whereas current research establishes that these algorithms can be improved upon in the worst case for the directed single-source variant.
Keywords: shortest path problem, graph algorithms, sorting barrier, computational complexity, k-disjoint paths
1. Introduction
The shortest path problem is a fundamental optimization task in computer science, essential for applications ranging from network routing to GPS navigation. According to Pagourtzis and Zachos [1], the concept is defined as the identification of a path between two nodes in a weighted graph that minimizes the total sum of edge weights. Both introductory treatments agree on the property of optimal substructure, where any subpath of a shortest path is itself a shortest path [1], [2]. Mehlhorn and Sanders [2] further emphasize the role of priority queues and edge relaxation as the core mechanisms for computational efficiency.
Introductory texts do not address recent developments concerning either combinatorial constraints on disjoint shortest paths or the best known time bounds for the single-source case on directed graphs. Chitnis, Thomas, and Wirth [3] review the k-Disjoint-Shortest-Paths problem and establish that all four variants (directed/undirected, edge/vertex) are W[1]-hard under ETH, with an inapproximability factor of (1/2 + ε) under Gap-ETH. Duan et al. [4] focus on directed single-source shortest paths and demonstrate that the problem can be solved faster than Dijkstra's O(m + n log n) time, which has long stood as the best known bound, by means of a Frontier Reduction technique. However, Chitnis et al. [3] note that the inapproximability factor for planar graphs has yet to be improved, and Duan et al. [4] acknowledge that large constant factors limit the practical impact of their algorithm. This paper therefore compares the shortest path concept across two coursebooks and two research articles, examining how its treatment develops from introductory to research level in terms of scope, methodology, and identified limitations.
2. Methods
A comparative analysis was conducted across four sources to examine how the shortest path concept is treated at introductory and research levels. Two coursebooks were selected from the bibliography of NTUA undergraduate computer science courses: Pagourtzis and Zachos [1], which presents the mathematical foundations of the problem, and Mehlhorn and Sanders [2], which presents its algorithmic implementation. A search for recent research was conducted in the Scopus database. Two queries were used: a direct query for Breaking the Sorting Barrier for Directed Single-Source Shortest Paths, and a broader query for "Shortest Path Problem," filtered for English-language, peer-reviewed sources in Computer Science, Engineering, and Mathematics. Three candidate papers, out of which two were selected, were compared on the basis of peer-review status, publication within the last six years, and relevance to the concept. The paper by Duan et al. [4] was selected as the research article on the basis of its recent publication, direct relevance to the time complexity of single-source shortest paths, and its claim of an improved time bound over the long-standing Dijkstra benchmark. The review article by Chitnis et al. [3] was selected to provide a complementary perspective on hardness and approximability for a constrained variant of the same concept. Each source was mapped along five dimensions: definition, methodological approach, key results, identified limitations, and treatment of the wider research context. 
3. Results
The two coursebooks define the shortest path problem in mutually consistent terms but develop the concept along different lines. Pagourtzis and Zachos [1] present the problem within a formal framework, moving from definition to proof of correctness, and emphasize the property of optimal substructure as the theoretical foundation of classical algorithms. Mehlhorn and Sanders [2] take the same definition as their starting point but develop it toward implementation, focusing on how data structures such as priority queues and the relaxation operation determine the asymptotic running time. Both works treat the problem on static graphs with both non-negative and real edge weights, and present Dijkstra's algorithm and Bellman–Ford as the standard solutions. Neither coursebook addresses constrained variants of the problem or the question of whether the classical time bounds for the single-source case can be improved.
The review article by Chitnis et al. [3] addresses a constrained variant of the shortest path concept, namely the k-Disjoint-Shortest-Paths problem, which requires k pairwise disjoint shortest paths between k terminal pairs. The authors apply a reduction framework from the k-Clique problem and use vertex-splitting operations to enforce disjointness. Their main results are that all four variants of the problem (directed/undirected, edge/vertex) are W[1]-hard under ETH, that a tight f(k)·n^o(k) lower bound holds, and that no (1/2 + ε)-approximation is possible under Gap-ETH. The authors identify the improvement of the inapproximability factor on planar graphs and the existence of an FPT algorithm for the directed vertex-disjoint case as open questions. 
The research article by Duan et al. [4] addresses the unconstrained single-source shortest path problem on directed graphs with non-negative edge weights and investigates whether the O(m + n log n) time of Dijkstra's algorithm — the best known bound for the problem for decades — can be improved. The authors propose a Frontier Reduction technique that combines recursive graph partitioning with Bellman–Ford-style relaxations to identify a small set of pivot vertices that drive the computation, thereby avoiding the global sort that Dijkstra's algorithm performs implicitly. The resulting deterministic algorithm runs in O(m·log^(2/3) n) time. The authors acknowledge that large constant factors limit the practical impact of the algorithm and that the result does not apply to graphs with negative edge weights and multiple sources. 
Taken together, the four sources address overlapping but distinct aspects of the shortest path concept. The coursebooks treat the canonical single-source problem on static graphs as a closed subject, where the running times of Dijkstra and Bellman–Ford are presented as the relevant benchmarks. The research literature treats the same canonical problem as still open — Duan et al. [4] improve on what the coursebooks present as the benchmark — while the review literature extends the concept to constrained variants whose computational difficulty is qualitatively different. 
4. Discussion
The four sources map a progression in the treatment of the shortest path problem from a settled algorithmic concept in the coursebooks [1], [2], to a constrained variant with hardness and approximability bounds in the review article [3], to a renewed investigation of the time complexity of the canonical case in the research paper [4]. The comparison suggests that the understanding of the shortest path concept remains incomplete in relation to both its computational limits and the difficulty of its constrained variants. This is consistent with the review article, which identifies the inapproximability factor on planar graphs and the existence of an FPT algorithm for the directed vertex-disjoint case as open questions [3], and with the research article, which demonstrates that even the canonical single-source case admits a faster algorithm than the long-standing O(m + n log n) benchmark [4]. As Duan et al. acknowledge, this result is limited to directed graphs with non-negative edge weights and a single source [4]; the mechanisms by which similar improvements might be obtained for other variants therefore remain only partially resolved.
Taken together, the four sources indicate that while the foundational definition and classical algorithms for the shortest path problem are well established, the optimal time complexity of even the canonical single-source case appears to remain incomplete and continues to be investigated in the research literature. However, this comparison is limited to four sources selected according to course criteria and cannot determine whether the same pattern is typical across the wider literature on graph algorithms. This does not affect the finding that, within the sources analysed, a bound that the coursebooks present as the benchmark has been improved upon by a result not represented in the introductory treatment.
The analysis provided us with insight that problems presented in introductory coursebooks as fully resolved may continue to evolve in the research literature. Although classical algorithms such as Dijkstra’s and Bellman–Ford are treated as established solutions, recent work demonstrates that accepted complexity bounds can still be challenged through the development of new algorithms and theoretical approaches.

Appendix C

Term 	Definition 
Shortest Path Problem 	A graph optimization problem that seeks the path between two nodes with the minimum total edge weight. 
Dijkstra’s Algorithm 	A classical algorithm used to compute shortest paths in graphs with non-negative edge weights, commonly treated as the standard benchmark for single-source shortest paths. 
Bellman–Ford Algorithm 	A shortest path algorithm capable of handling graphs with negative edge weights through repeated edge relaxation operations. 
Computational Complexity 	The study of the computational resources, mainly time and memory, required for solving algorithmic problems. In the paper, it is used to compare algorithmic efficiency and hardness results. 
Edge Relaxation 	An operation used in shortest path algorithms where the current best-known distance to a node is updated if a shorter path is found through another edge. 
k-Disjoint Shortest Paths 	A constrained variant of the shortest path problem requiring k pairwise disjoint shortest paths between terminal pairs in a graph.
